## Lecture 2.1: Life Expectancy

### Lifespan vs. Life Expectancy
- **Lifespan** = how long ONE individual lives
- **Life expectancy (e0)** = the AVERAGE length of life in a population (average of everyone's lifespans)
  - e0 = life expectancy at birth
  - e65 = life expectancy at age 65
  - etc.

### How Life Expectancy is Calculated
Just like TFR, life expectancy is a **period measure** based on a synthetic cohort. It asks: "If a baby born today experienced today's age-specific mortality rates for their entire life, how long would they live on average?"

This means it has the same weakness as TFR -- it's a snapshot, not a prediction. It doesn't actually tell you how long you'll live. **Cohort life expectancy** (tracking an actual birth cohort through their lives) is more accurate but requires waiting for everyone to die.

Key insight: If mortality is declining over time, **cohort life expectancy is typically LARGER than period life expectancy**, because conditions keep improving as the cohort ages.

### Why Infant Mortality Matters So Much
Infant deaths pull down e0 dramatically because they represent ~80 lost years each. The slides gave a great example:
- 9 people die at ages 65-89: average = 76 years
- Add ONE infant death (age 1): average drops to 68.3 years

This is why **before 1950**, life expectancy gains came mainly from reducing infant/child mortality. **After 1950**, gains shifted to improved survival at older ages.

### Centenarians and Super-Centenarians
- **Centenarian** = lives to 100+
- **Super-centenarian** = lives to 110+
- The big question: is there a hard limit to human lifespan? Debate is ongoing.

### Compression of Mortality
As medicine improves, deaths get concentrated into a narrower age range (mostly old age). The survival curve starts looking like a rectangle -- most people survive to old age, then die within a relatively short window. This is called **rectangularization** of the survival curve.

### Compression of Morbidity
The hopeful hypothesis: as life expectancy increases, the period of sickness/disability before death gets SHORTER, not longer. People live longer AND healthier, with illness compressed into a brief window at the end.

**Definition for exam:** "Increased life expectancy will be accompanied by a shortening of the length of time spent in poor health."

The opposite would be **expansion of morbidity** -- living longer but spending more years sick.

### Healthy Life Expectancy
Expected years of life lived in **good health**, measured by functional limitations (can you stand, get dressed, prepare food?). **Morbidity** = prevalence of disease in a population.

### US Life Expectancy: The Bad News
- US life expectancy **lags** comparable rich countries
- US life expectancy even lags some much POORER countries
- Recent trends have been especially bad (more on this in 2.3)

### Recent Life Expectancy Gains
True or False: "Recent increases in life expectancy have depended on increased survival among the older adult population." **TRUE.** Early gains came from infant mortality reduction; recent gains come from keeping old people alive longer.

## Lecture 2.2: Population Aging

### Three Types of Age

| Type | Definition | Vibe |
|------|-----------|------|
| **Chronological age** | Calendar years alive | "How old are you?" |
| **Biological age** | Physiological condition based on biomarkers (metabolic, cardiovascular, inflammatory, etc.) | "You look good for your age!" |
| **Sociological age** | Society's expectations for someone of a given age | "You shouldn't do that at your age!" |
| **Subjective age** | Your own perception of how old you feel | "I should have done X by now!" |

Biological age is measured from biomarkers (telomere length is one measure) and is influenced by environment (pollution), behavior (smoking), genetics, and random variation. Two people who are both chronologically 50 can have very different biological ages.

### Three Ways to Measure Population Aging

1. **Median age** -- line everyone up, find the middle person's age. US median age is ~39.2.
2. **Share of population 65+** -- US is about 14.9% now, projected to hit 22.1%
3. **Healthy life expectancy** -- are we compressing or expanding morbidity?

### What Causes Population Aging?
Three things:
1. **Improved survival at older ages** (people live longer)
2. **Declining fertility** (fewer young people coming in at the bottom)
3. **Migration** (immigrants are typically working age; out-migration from rural areas leaves older populations behind)

### Aging Effect vs. Longevity Effect

| Effect | What's Happening | Impact on Health |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Aging effect** | Population gets chronologically older | More disease and death (just because people are older) |
| **Longevity effect** | People are biologically healthier at the same chronological age ("75 is the new 65") | LESS disease at any given age |

These two effects can **offset** each other. The Alzheimer's example from the slides: crude rates of Alzheimer's went up (aging effect), but age-standardized rates stayed flat since 1990 (the increase was just because there were more old people, not because Alzheimer's became more common).

### The Old Age Dependency Ratio (OADR)
```
OADR = Population 65+ / Population 15-64
```
A high OADR means fewer working-age people supporting more retirees. This stresses:
- **Healthcare** -- fewer workers paying taxes to fund Medicare/Medicaid
- **Social Security** -- fewer contributors, more beneficiaries
- **Caregiving** -- fewer people available to provide care
- **Economy** -- potentially slower growth, less innovation

China's example from the slides is dramatic:
- 2010: 5.4 working-age adults per elderly person
- 2030: 2.5
- 2050: 1.6

That's a "demographic implosion."

### "Demographic Time Bomb"
The alarming (maybe overly alarming) term for what happens when a country's dependency ratio spikes. The idea is that the system becomes unsustainable.

### Age Inflation
As life expectancy increases, the meaning of "old" changes. Someone who is 65 today is biologically more like a 55-year-old from 50 years ago. So using a fixed age threshold (like 65) to define "old" makes populations look more "aged" than they functionally are.

### Natural Decrease
When deaths exceed births in a population. This is already happening in many rural US counties and will become more common as the population ages.

### Potential Solutions to Population Aging
1. Encourage work in later life (raise retirement age)
2. Reduce pensions
3. Labor and finance policy reform
4. Increase immigration (immigrants are typically working age)
5. Increase fertility (pro-natalist policies)

### Four Considerations About These Solutions
1. **Pace** -- How urgent is the problem? Where will it hit first?
2. **Socioeconomic inequality** -- Do solutions hit different groups differently?
3. **Geography** -- Where are the old people? (Rural areas age faster because young people leave)
4. **Generational equity** -- Age-based policies redistribute resources between cohorts. Changing policies mid-stream can penalize specific cohorts.

## Lecture 2.3: Life Expectancy and Social Policy

### US Health Reversals
Since ~2000, mid-life mortality has been INCREASING in the US. Life expectancy actually decreased in 2016 and 2017. It recovered slightly in 2018-2019, then COVID crushed it again.

### "Deaths of Despair"
Term coined by Case & Deaton for the three causes driving mid-life mortality increases:
1. **Drug overdoses** (especially opioids)
2. **Alcohol-attributable mortality**
3. **Suicide**

These are concentrated among **white Americans without a college degree**. The education gap in life expectancy has widened while the racial gap has narrowed (because white non-college mortality got worse).

### Key Insight from the Readings
Life expectancy in adulthood is falling for those without a BA degree. Education has become one of the strongest predictors of how long you'll live in America.

### (Re)emerging Infectious Diseases
COVID-19 is the obvious example, but the broader point is that infectious diseases haven't been "conquered." New pathogens and resurgences of old ones (like measles in under-vaccinated communities) remain threats to population health.

### Why is US Health So Bad Compared to Other Rich Countries?
The US spends more on healthcare than any other country but has worse outcomes. Contributing factors include:
- Higher inequality
- Weaker social safety net
- More gun violence
- Higher rates of obesity
- Less universal healthcare access
- The opioid crisis

## Concepts to Know -- Quick Flashcard Style

**Human lifespan** -- The length of an individual life

**Centenarian** -- Someone who lives to 100+

**Super-centenarian** -- Someone who lives to 110+

**Life expectancy** -- Average length of life in a population (period measure)

**Healthy life expectancy** -- Expected years lived in good health

**Compression of mortality** -- Deaths concentrated into a narrower, older age range

**Compression of morbidity** -- Period of poor health before death gets shorter

**Chronological age** -- Calendar years alive

**Biological age** -- Physiological condition measured by biomarkers

**Sociological age** -- Society's expectations for someone of a given age

**Subjective age** -- How old you feel

**OADR** -- Old age dependency ratio (65+ / 15-64)

**Demographic time bomb** -- Unsustainable spike in dependency ratios

**Aging effect** -- More disease because population is chronologically older

**Longevity effect** -- Less disease because people are biologically younger at the same chronological age

**Age inflation** -- "Old" means something different than it used to

**Natural decrease** -- Deaths exceeding births

**Deaths of despair** -- Drug overdoses, alcohol, suicide (Case & Deaton)

**Health reversals** -- When mortality trends reverse and start getting worse
