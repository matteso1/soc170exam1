## Lecture 1.1: What is Population Thinking?

### The Big Idea
Demography is the study of populations -- not individuals. You're looking at the forest, not the trees. A demographer doesn't care that *you* had a kid; they care that the birth *rate* changed.

### What Makes a Population Important?
Four things:
1. **Size** -- how many people
2. **Composition** -- who those people are (age, sex, race, etc.)
3. **Distribution** -- where they are (this is where cartograms come in)
4. **Dynamics** -- how the population is changing over time

### Population Cartograms
A map where country size is based on **population**, not land area. So India and China look massive, and Canada/Australia shrink. A small country with a huge population density (like Bangladesh) **increases** in size on a cartogram.

### The Balancing Equation
This is the fundamental equation of demography. Memorize it:

```
P(t2) = P(t1) + Births - Deaths + Immigration - Emigration + error
```

- **Natural increase** = Births - Deaths
- **Net migration** = Immigration - Emigration
- **Error of closure** = the leftover imbalance when the numbers don't perfectly add up (because data is messy)

### Counts, Ratios, and Rates
These three are different and the exam WILL test you on it:

| Type | What it is | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Count** | Raw number of events or people | "147,890 births in Pennsylvania" |
| **Ratio** | One subgroup compared to another subgroup or to the total | "There are 95 men for every 100 women" |
| **Rate** | Events divided by population at risk, over a time period | "12.1 births per 1,000 people per year" |

### Crude Rates vs Better Rates
A **crude rate** uses the total population as the denominator. Problem: not everyone is "at risk." Babies can't get married, men can't give birth, etc.

**Person-years of exposure** is a better denominator -- it accounts for how long each person was actually in the population and at risk of the event. Think of it as: if someone lived in the population for only 6 months, they contribute 0.5 person-years.

### Why Crude Rates Can Mislead
Japan has a HIGHER crude death rate than the US (11.7 vs 8.4) but a HIGHER life expectancy (85 vs 77). Why? Japan's population is older, so more people die per year just because there are more old people. When you adjust for age, Japan is way healthier.

## Lecture 1.2: Population Pyramids and Age Structure

### Age, Period, Cohort (APC)
Three concepts that keep coming back all semester:
- **Age** = how old someone is
- **Period** = a specific time (usually a year)
- **Cohort** = a group born at the same time

The math relationship: **Cohort = Period - Age** (born in 2000 = period 2024 - age 24)

### Population Pyramids
A bar chart on its side. Males on the left, females on the right, age groups stacked vertically. Each bar shows the **number or percent of males and females of a given age**.

### Why Age Structure Matters (4 Reasons)
1. **Record of past events** -- wars, baby booms, famines show up as dents or bulges
2. **Who is at risk** -- lots of 15-49 year old women? Expect more births
3. **Potential for growth** -- wide base = growing population; narrow base = shrinking
4. **Dependency ratios** -- how many non-workers vs workers

### The Baby Boom and Echo
The **baby boom** (1946-1964) created a huge bulge in the US pyramid. When boomers had kids, that created the **baby boom echo** (their children). The echo is literally the population of children born to the baby boom cohort.

### Dependency Ratios
```
Old Age Dependency Ratio (OADR) = Population 65+ / Population 15-64
```
High OADR = fewer workers supporting more retirees = strain on healthcare and social security.

### Demographic Dividend
When a country's dependency ratio drops (lots of working-age people, fewer dependents), the economy booms. China experienced this big time. But the flip side is coming -- a **demographic implosion** as dependency ratios spike back up.

### Types of Pyramids
- **Young** (wide base, triangle shape) = growing fast
- **Mature** (more rectangular) = stable
- **Old/Declining** (narrow base, bulge at top) = shrinking

## Lecture 1.3: How Do We Know What We Know?

### Sources of Population Data
| Source | What it measures |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Census** | Full count of population (every 10 years in US, mandated by Constitution) |
| **Vital registration** | Birth certificates, death certificates |
| **Immigration records** | Visas, official entries |
| **Surveys** | American Community Survey, Current Population Survey, etc. |

### Why the Census Exists (Constitutional reasons)
1. **Apportionment** -- allocating seats in the House of Representatives to states
2. **Taxation + spending** -- distributing federal funds

Note: **determining tax rates** is NOT an official use of Census data (this was on the practice exam).

### The Undercount Problem
An **undercount** = when certain sub-populations are systematically missed by the Census. Groups most likely to be undercounted:
- Black Americans (historically the worst undercount)
- Hispanic Americans (~5% undercount = ~3 million people missing)
- Young children, elderly, people who move frequently
- People who avoid government systems (undocumented immigrants, etc.)

### The Citizenship Question Controversy (2020 Census)
The main concern was that adding a citizenship question would scare immigrant families (documented AND undocumented) into not responding or responding inaccurately, making the undercount even worse. This has real consequences for representation in Congress and federal funding.

### Error of Closure
When you plug all your data into the balancing equation and the numbers don't match up perfectly, the gap is the **error of closure**. It exists because no data source is perfect.

## Lecture 1.4: Demographic Transitions

### First Demographic Transition (FDT)
The classic pattern that every modernizing country goes through:

| Stage | Birth Rate | Death Rate | Population |
|-------|-----------|------------|------------|
| 1 (Pre-modern) | High | High | Stable |
| 2 (Early transition) | High | **Falling** | **Growing** |
| 3 (Late transition) | **Falling** | Low | Still growing |
| 4 (Post-transition) | Low | Low | Stable |
| 5 (Some countries) | Very low | Low | Shrinking |

**Key:** Mortality declines FIRST (because of health improvements), fertility stays high, so population booms. Then fertility catches up and declines too.

Why does fertility eventually fall? Families no longer need many kids to ensure some survive. It becomes more advantageous to invest resources in fewer kids.

### Second Demographic Transition (SDT)
A newer theory (Van de Kaa, 1987) about what happens AFTER the first transition in wealthy countries:

1. **Marriage weakens** as the only family form (divorce rises, cohabitation increases)
2. Shift from **"king-child"** to **"king-couple"** -- parents prioritize their own well-being, not just sacrificing everything for kids
3. Contraception shifts from **preventive** (avoiding unwanted births) to **self-fulfilling** (having kids as personal choice/expression)
4. **Pluralistic family forms** emerge (single parents, cohabiting couples, blended families, etc.)

**Result:** sustained sub-replacement fertility, diverse living arrangements, disconnection between marriage and procreation, and NO stationary population (it keeps changing).

The SDT is NOT limited to Europe/US -- aspects of it are visible worldwide (Mexico, China, etc.). But don't assume every country will follow the exact same path ("reading history sideways").

### Maslow's Theory of Changing Needs
As societies develop, people move from basic survival needs to self-actualization. This connects to the SDT: once survival is secured, people prioritize personal fulfillment over maximizing family size.

## Lecture 1.5: Malthus and the Population Debate

### Thomas Malthus (1798)
His argument was simple:
- Population grows **exponentially** (2, 4, 8, 16...)
- Food production grows **arithmetically** (2, 3, 4, 5...)
- Eventually population outstrips food supply = famine and mass death

His proposed solutions:
- **Preventative checks** = moral restraint (delayed marriage, abstinence, restricting marriage of the poor). NOT restraints on consumption -- this was a practice exam question.
- **Positive checks** = the bad stuff that happens when you don't restrain (disease, starvation, war)

### Was Malthus Right?
Not really. Despite massive population growth, global poverty RATES have declined. We produce enough food. But the idea is seductive because it feels intuitively true.

### Neo-Malthusians
People who recycle Malthus's argument in modern contexts (Paul Ehrlich's "The Population Bomb" etc.)

### Three Critiques of Malthus

| Critique | Core Argument |
|----------|--------------|
| **Moral/Inequality** | Hunger and poverty are caused by **unequal distribution** of resources, NOT by population growth itself. There IS enough food; it's just wasted or inaccessible. |
| **Technological** | Human innovation solves scarcity. Resource scarcity drives up prices, which incentivizes finding alternatives. (Simon vs. Ehrlich bet -- Simon won.) The Green Revolution tripled food production. |
| **Demographic** | Population growth is temporary (demographic transition). Growth rates vary over time. Don't extrapolate to infinity. |

**IMPORTANT distinction:** "Social inequality interferes with technology" is the INEQUALITY critique, NOT the technological critique. The tech critique is optimistic about human innovation.

### Population Momentum
Even when the birth RATE drops, the total number of births can keep increasing if there are lots of people of reproductive age. Think of it this way: if 100 women each have 1 baby, that's more total babies than 2 women each having 50 babies -- even though the rate is way lower.

The slides had a great example:
- Time 1: 2 young people have 100 babies (rate = 50). Population = 104.
- Time 2: 100 young people have 100 babies (rate = 1). Population = 202.
- The rate CRASHED but the population nearly DOUBLED.

Global population growth rate peaked ~1965, but the largest absolute number of people added peaked ~1985. That lag is momentum.

### Elon Musk and "Population Doom"
The opposite of Malthus -- worrying about too FEW people. Same critiques apply in reverse (moral: forcing people to have babies is bad; technological: IVF, childcare policy could help; demographic: extrapolating current trends to infinity is foolish).

## Lecture 1.6: Population and Environment

### The IPAT Equation
```
I = P x A x T
```
- **I** = Human Impact on environment
- **P** = Population size
- **A** = Affluence (per capita consumption)
- **T** = Technology

Population is only ONE part of the equation. Affluence and technology matter too (often more).

Key insight from the slides: "It is more accurate to say that *consumers*, rather than *people*, cause climate change." (Stephenson et al. 2010)

### The "Washing Machine Line" and "Air Line" (Hans Rosling)
Rosling categorized global wealth into tiers based on what people can afford:
- **Washing machine line** = the level of wealth where people can afford a washing machine (replacing manual labor with machines). This is the threshold where consumption starts really increasing.
- **Air line** = the level of wealth where people can afford air travel. This is the highest tier of resource consumption.

Most environmental impact comes from people above the air line. The billions below the washing machine line contribute very little to environmental damage.

### Social Construction of Scarcity
Resources aren't inherently "scarce" -- scarcity is often created by how societies distribute and waste resources. There's enough food for everyone; the problem is distribution, not production.

### Climate and Population -- What Do We Know?
- **Fertility:** Very small effects from climate change
- **Mortality:** Small relative to global population, but disasters matter
- **Migration:** BIG impacts. Climate displacement is real and growing. 60% of world's largest cities are in low-lying coastal areas.

## Lecture 1.7: Fewer Babies or Later Babies?

### Key Definitions
- **Fertility** = number of children actually born
- **Fecundity** = biological *capacity* to produce a live birth
- **Infertility** = reduced ability to conceive (medical definition: failure after 12+ months of trying)

### Measures of Fertility (know these!)

| Measure | Formula | Type |
|---------|---------|------|
| **Crude Fertility Rate (CFR)** | # births / total population | Period |
| **General Fertility Rate (GFR)** | # births / # women aged 15-49 | Period |
| **Age-Specific Fertility Rate (ASFR)** | # births to women age X / # women age X | Period |
| **Total Fertility Rate (TFR)** | Sum of all ASFRs (x5 if using 5-year groups) | Period |
| **Cohort Completed Fertility** | Total births to a cohort by end of reproductive years | Cohort |

**TFR** is the most commonly cited but it has a BIG flaw:

### Tempo vs. Quantum (this is important!)
- **Tempo** = the TIMING of births
- **Quantum** = the NUMBER of births

If women just DELAY having kids (tempo shift), the TFR drops temporarily even if they'll eventually have the same number of kids. This is called the **tempo effect** or **postponement transition**.

**Cohort completed fertility** is more "real" because it measures what actually happened over a lifetime. But the downside is you have to wait until women finish having kids to calculate it.

In the US, completed cohort fertility tells a **different picture** than TFR -- cohort fertility has been more stable, suggesting some of the TFR decline is just postponement.

### Period vs. Cohort Measures

| | Period | Cohort |
|--|--------|--------|
| **Pro** | Timely (available now) | Accurate (measures real outcomes) |
| **Con** | May not be accurate (tempo effects) | Not timely (retrospective, have to wait) |

### Age and Fecundity
- Fecundity declines with age, accelerating in late 30s/early 40s
- BUT historical data shows women routinely had babies into their 40s
- Often it just takes more trying at older ages
- The declining US birth rate is primarily driven by declining teen births and births to 20-24 year olds, not older women stopping

## Lecture 1.8: Family Planning and Unmet Need

### Why Does Fertility Decline? Two Theories

| Theory | Core Idea |
|--------|-----------|
| **Demand theory** | Economic development increases the "cost" of children, so people demand fewer kids. "Development is the best contraceptive." |
| **Diffusion theory** | Ideas about smaller families spread through social networks, media, and family planning programs. Programs change norms and increase access. |

**Exam distinction:** "Socio-economic development creates demand for lower fertility" = DEMAND theory, NOT diffusion. Diffusion is about the spread of ideas and access through programs/messaging.

### Coale's Three Prerequisites for Fertility Decline
1. **Ready** -- fertility must be within conscious choice
2. **Willing** -- reduced fertility must be seen as advantageous
3. **Able** -- effective contraceptive techniques must be available

### The Three Big International Conferences

| Conference | Key Theme |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Bucharest (1974)** | Rich countries push family planning; poor countries push back saying "development is the best contraceptive" |
| **Mexico City (1984)** | Countries that limited fertility saw a "demographic dividend"; US supports economic growth perspective |
| **Cairo (1994)** | Shift from coercive "family planning" to rights-based "human development"; focus on education + women's empowerment |

### Unmet Need for Contraception
**Definition:** The proportion of women who want to delay or stop childbearing but are NOT currently using contraception. It's the gap between demand and use.

**Sub-Saharan Africa** has the lowest overall demand for contraception but the HIGHEST level of unmet need.

### Why Don't Women Use Contraception?
The #1 reason in lower-income countries is NOT lack of access (that was true in the 1960s but not anymore). The most common reasons now:
- **Concerns about side effects** (this is the big one)
- Infrequent sex (increasingly common in Southeast Asia)
- Opposition from partners/family
- Noneconomic costs (stigma, travel time, partner disapproval) absolutely DO affect access

### US Policy (Lecture 1.9 overlap)
- **Helms Amendment (1973):** No US foreign aid money for abortion
- **Mexico City Policy / "Global Gag Rule":** NGOs receiving US family planning money can't even MENTION abortion, even with their own non-US funds. Gets toggled on/off by every new president (Republicans reinstate, Democrats rescind)
- **Replacement level fertility** = ~2.1 children per woman. US has been BELOW this since 2007.

## Lecture 1.9: How Low is US Fertility?

### Key Concepts
- **Sub-replacement fertility** = TFR below ~2.1 (the level needed to replace the population without immigration)
- **Low fertility** = TFR below 2.1
- **Lowest-low fertility** = TFR below 1.3 (seen in some Southern/Eastern European and East Asian countries)
- **Parity** = the number of children a woman has had (parity 0 = no kids, parity 1 = one kid, etc.)

### The Two-Child Norm
In most high-income countries, couples say they WANT about two children, even as actual fertility falls below 2. This "two-child norm" is remarkably persistent. But research shows preferences for 2 kids aren't actually that much stronger than 0, 1, or 3 when you account for work, finances, and relationship status.

### US Fertility Facts
- US TFR was below replacement in 2021 (True -- practice exam question)
- The US is still in a period of **natural increase** (births > deaths) because of population momentum
- US fertility differentials: fertility varies by race, education, income, geography

### Potential Consequences of Sustained Low Fertility
- Shrinking population
- More old people than young people (population aging)
- NOT imbalanced sex ratios (that's caused by sex-selective practices or migration, not low fertility)

### Foregone Fertility
The gap between how many kids people say they want and how many they actually have. People "forgo" births due to financial constraints, career pressures, relationship instability, etc.
