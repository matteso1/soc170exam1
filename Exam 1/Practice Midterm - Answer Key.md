## Q1: Unmet need for contraception occurs when...

- **the demand for contraception is higher than the use of contraception**
- ~~policy makers want women to use contraception more than women themselves want to use contraception~~
- ~~more forms of family planning are available than women and their partners are willing to use~~

> Unmet need exists when there is a gap between the desire to avoid pregnancy and actual contraceptive use. (Lecture 1.8)

## Q2: True or False: Infrequent sex is an increasingly common reason for not using contraception in Southeast Asia.

- ~~False~~

## Q3: What is unmet need for family planning?

- ~~The lack of access to contraception at the moment that one is ready to have sex~~
- ~~The amount by which contraceptive use would need to increase in order for fertility to decline to two births per woman~~

> This is the standard demographic definition of unmet need. (Lecture 1.8)

## Q5: True or false: Noneconomic costs do not affect access to contraception.

## Q6: Which of the following statements is true about unmet need and desire for contraception in sub-Saharan Africa?

## Q7: What is a cohort?

- **The population sub-unit that experiences a demographic event during a specific time interval**
- ~~The population sub-group that experiences a demographic event most frequently~~
- ~~The base population unit used to compare countries~~

> A cohort is defined by shared experience of a demographic event (e.g., birth, marriage) within a specific time period. (Lecture 1.2)

## Q8: What is the first demographic transition?

- **A sequence of population change: mortality declines first, followed by fertility decline**
- ~~The change from low-population density to high-population density as a population grows~~
- ~~The historical shift in the methods of measuring and analyzing demographic data~~

> The first demographic transition describes the shift from high mortality/high fertility to low mortality/low fertility, with mortality declining first. (Lecture 1.4)

## Q9: What is an undercount?

- ~~When statistics are derived from a sample of the population rather than the full Census count~~
- **When certain sub-populations are more likely to be left out of the Census count than other groups**
- ~~When the government allocates fewer Representatives to a state than its enumerated population is warranted~~

> An undercount refers to the systematic missing of certain groups (racial minorities, immigrants, homeless populations) in the Census. (Lecture 1.3)

## Q10: Which of the following is NOT consistent with the technological critique of Malthus?

- **Social inequality interferes with the implementation of technological innovations**
- ~~A larger population means more people and labor to work out technological solutions to resource scarcity~~
- ~~Population pressure encourages agricultural productivity and the intensification of food production~~

> The technological critique argues that human innovation can overcome resource scarcity. The idea that social inequality interferes with technology is more aligned with the inequality critique. (Lecture 1.5)

## Q11: In the early stage of the demographic transition, mortality declines first while fertility remains high. This leads to:

- ~~declining, then increasing population~~
- **population increase**
- ~~stable population~~
- ~~population decline~~

> When mortality drops but fertility stays high, there are more births than deaths, causing population growth. (Lecture 1.4)

## Q16: What is the balancing equation of population change?

- ~~A way of calculating whether the earth can sustain its current rate of population growth~~
- **A way of calculating changes in population size based on the magnitude of flows into or out of a population**
- ~~A way of calculating the number of men and women in a population are balanced~~

> The balancing equation: P(t+1) = P(t) + Births - Deaths + Immigration - Emigration. (Lecture 1.1)

## Q17: Which is not an example of the "diffusion theory" of fertility?

- **socio-economic development creates demand for lower fertility and drives contraceptive use.**
- ~~Family planning programs' messaging campaigns influencing the fertility preferences of women and their partners~~
- ~~Family planning programs increase affordability and access to contraceptives.~~

> Socio-economic development driving demand is a "demand theory" explanation, not diffusion. Diffusion theory focuses on the spread of ideas and access through programs and social networks. (Lecture 1.8)

## Q18: What is population momentum?

- **The number of births may continue to increase--even if fertility levels are lower--if the number of reproductive-aged adults continues to grow**
- ~~When population size exceeds food production, environmental and social catastrophe will lead to a population crash~~
- ~~Once a population is increasing, it is not possible to stop or slow down population growth~~

> Population momentum occurs because a large cohort of young people will produce many births even at lower per-person fertility rates. (Lecture 1.5)

## Q19: Thomas Malthus believed that restraints on consumption was the solution to an impending population crisis?

- ~~True~~
- **False**

> Malthus advocated for "preventative checks" -- moral restraint on reproduction (delayed marriage, abstinence) -- not restraints on consumption. (Lecture 1.5)

---

## Q20: What is the compression of morbidity hypothesis?

- **Increased life expectancy will be accompanied by a shortening of the length of time spent in poor health**
- ~~Poor health will become concentrated among a smaller share of the population~~
- ~~Poor health will become more common at younger ages and less common at older ages~~

> Compression of morbidity means that as we live longer, the period of illness/disability gets squeezed into a shorter window before death. (Lecture 2.1)

---

## Q21: True or False: Demographers are primarily concerned with the consequences of individual-level behavior for individuals.

- ~~True~~
- **False**

> Demographers study population-level patterns and processes, not individual outcomes. Population thinking is about aggregate consequences. (Lecture 1.1)

## Q27: How might higher dependency ratios stress a nation's healthcare infrastructure?

## Q28: True or False: According to the inequality critique of Malthus, population growth and environmental decline cause hunger and poverty.

- ~~True~~
- **False**

> The inequality critique argues the reverse: it is social inequality and unequal distribution of resources -- not population growth itself -- that causes hunger and poverty. (Lecture 1.5)

## Q29: In the US,

- ~~cohort completed fertility has been declining in recent years.~~
- ~~we do not collect enough data to estimate the total fertility rate accurately.~~
- ~~the total fertility rate is a more reliable measure of fertility than completed cohort fertility.~~
- **completed cohort fertility tells a different picture than the total fertility rate.**

> TFR is a period measure affected by tempo effects (postponement), while completed cohort fertility captures actual lifetime births. In the US, cohort fertility has been more stable than the declining TFR suggests. (Lecture 1.7, 1.9)

## Q30: Which of the following is not a potential consequence of sustained low fertility in the United States?

- ~~Shrinking population~~
- **Imbalanced population sex ratios**
- ~~Population with more old than young people~~

> Low fertility leads to population decline and aging, but does not inherently cause sex ratio imbalances (that is driven by sex-selective practices or migration patterns). (Lecture 1.9)
