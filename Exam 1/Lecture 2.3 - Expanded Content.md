## Lecture Summary

Since 2000, mid-life mortality rates have been increasing in the United States, which caused life expectancy to decrease in 2016 and 2017. Although life expectancy rose in 2018 and 2019, the COVID-19 pandemic has reversed these gains. This lecture examines:
- What causes health reversals?
- Have recent health reversals been evenly distributed, or have some sub-groups been more affected?
- Why is health in the US so much worse than in other rich countries?

---

## Key Reading: Case & Deaton (2021)

**"Life expectancy in adulthood is falling for those without a BA degree, but as educational gaps have widened, racial gaps have narrowed."** PNAS 118(11).

### Main Findings:
- Life expectancy at age 25 has been **falling** for Americans without a BA degree
- The **education gap** in mortality has been **widening** -- college-educated Americans are pulling away
- Meanwhile, **racial gaps** in life expectancy have been **narrowing** -- not because Black mortality improved dramatically, but because white non-college mortality got significantly worse
- The causes are concentrated in **mid-life mortality** (ages 25-64), driven by:
  - Drug overdoses (especially opioids)
  - Alcohol-related liver disease
  - Suicide
- These three causes are collectively termed **"deaths of despair"**
- Deaths of despair are concentrated among white Americans without a college degree
- The geographic pattern: worst in Appalachia, the Rust Belt, and rural America

### Implications:
- Education has become one of the strongest predictors of how long you will live in America
- The BA degree has become a dividing line for health outcomes
- This is not primarily a racial story -- it cuts across racial lines but is stratified by education

## Key Reading: Preston & Vierboom (The Guardian)

**"Why do Americans die earlier than Europeans?"**

### Main Arguments:
- Americans have shorter life expectancy than Europeans despite spending far more on healthcare
- Contributing factors include:
  - Higher rates of gun violence
  - Higher rates of drug overdose deaths (opioid epidemic)
  - Higher rates of obesity
  - More traffic fatalities
  - Greater income inequality
  - Weaker social safety net (less universal healthcare, less paid leave, less social housing)
  - Higher rates of poverty, especially child poverty
- The US health disadvantage is not limited to the poor -- even wealthy, insured Americans have worse outcomes than their European counterparts on many measures
- The US healthcare system is good at treating acute conditions but poor at prevention and managing chronic disease

## Concepts to Know - Detailed Definitions

### Health Reversals
When mortality trends that had been improving reverse course and start getting worse. The US experienced this starting around 2000, particularly in mid-life ages. Life expectancy declined in 2016, 2017, and then dramatically during COVID.

### Alcohol-Attributable Mortality
Deaths caused by alcohol use, including liver disease (cirrhosis), alcohol poisoning, alcohol-related accidents, and other causes. Rising significantly in the US, especially among middle-aged adults.

### Drug Overdoses
The leading driver of increasing mid-life mortality in the US. The opioid epidemic has been the biggest factor, evolving through three waves:
1. Prescription opioids (starting in the 1990s)
2. Heroin (starting around 2010)
3. Synthetic opioids like fentanyl (starting around 2013)
Drug overdose deaths hit over 100,000 per year in the US by 2021.

### Mid-Life Mortality
Mortality among working-age adults (roughly ages 25-64). This is the age range where the US has seen the most alarming increases, and where the US most dramatically underperforms compared to peer countries.

### (Re)emerging Infectious Diseases
Infectious diseases that were thought to be controlled but have returned or emerged anew. COVID-19 is the primary example, wiping out years of life expectancy gains. But also includes resurgences of measles in under-vaccinated communities, drug-resistant tuberculosis, etc. The point: infectious disease has not been "conquered."

### "Deaths of Despair"
Term coined by economists Anne Case and Angus Deaton. Refers to three specific causes of death that have been rising dramatically:
1. Drug overdoses
2. Alcohol-related liver disease and poisoning
3. Suicide

These are called "deaths of despair" because they are linked to economic distress, social disconnection, loss of community, declining job prospects, and the erosion of institutions that once provided meaning and stability (unions, churches, stable employment). They are concentrated among white Americans without a college degree and in communities that have experienced economic decline (deindustrialization, job loss).

## Connections to Other Lectures

- **Lecture 2.1 (Life Expectancy):** 2.3 builds directly on the concept of life expectancy as a period measure and asks why the US is an outlier among rich countries.
- **Lecture 2.2 (Population Aging):** Health reversals interact with population aging -- if mid-life mortality increases, fewer people survive to old age, which affects the age structure and dependency ratios.

- **Lecture 1.4 (Demographic Transitions):** The US health reversal is a departure from the expected pattern of the demographic transition, where mortality is supposed to keep declining.
